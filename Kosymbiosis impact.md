Here’s my honest take on that idea.

1️⃣ Technically, the analogy actually makes sense
AI already works somewhat like this:

When you use models from companies like OpenAI or Anthropic, you are charged per token or per API call.

That’s basically metered compute usage.

The reason is that running large AI models requires huge infrastructure (GPUs, data centers, electricity). 

So the idea of “intelligence as infrastructure” isn’t crazy.
It’s similar to how we consume:

electricity

cloud computing

internet bandwidth

AI could become another utility-like layer of the digital economy.

2️⃣ But the comparison also raises real concerns
Utilities are essential services. If AI becomes as important as electricity, some problems appear:

⚠️ Power concentration
If a few companies control AI infrastructure:

OpenAI

Google

Microsoft

they could effectively control access to “digital intelligence”.

That’s why some critics say the idea sounds dystopian. 

⚠️ Access inequality
Altman himself warned that limited computing capacity could drive prices up, meaning only wealthy users or companies might get large amounts of AI power. 

That could create something like:

“AI rich”

“AI poor”

⚠️ Massive energy demand
AI requires huge compute infrastructure and electricity.

Even industry leaders warn that power generation and data centers may become the main bottleneck for AI growth. 

3️⃣ My overall opinion
The idea is probably realistic but incomplete.

Likely true:

AI will become infrastructure like cloud computing.

People will pay per use in many contexts.

But unlikely to be the only model:

open-source AI will exist

local AI models will run on personal devices

governments may regulate it like utilities.

So the future is probably a mix of:

utility AI (big cloud models)

local AI on devices

open-source models.

Altman’s statement isn’t crazy—it reflects how AI infrastructure actually works—but the “utility” framing raises big questions about control, regulation, and access.
